## Overview

Sophie Germain is the standard way to factor a sum of two fourth powers when
one coefficient is $4$. It mostly appears in AIME and is usually an intermediate step

## Key Ideas

- $x^4 + 4y^4 = (x^2 - 2xy + 2y^2)(x^2 + 2xy + 2y^2)$.
- Think of it as $(x^2 + 2y^2)^2 - (2xy)^2$.
- It often appears in AIME-level factoring.

## Core Skills

### Confirm the Coefficient

The identity applies only when the coefficient of $y^4$ is $4$. Factor out
constants first to match this pattern. This can be useful to perform cancellations and to avoid messy calculations which can cost valuable time

### Use Difference of Squares

Recognize $x^4+4y^4$ as $(x^2)^2+(2y^2)^2$. Then $x^4+4y^4=(x^2)^2+(2y^2)^2+4x^2y^2-(4xy)^2=(x^2+2y^2-4xy)(x^2+2y^2+4xy)$

### Look for Substitutions

If the expression is not in the exact form, set $x$ or $y$ as a binomial
to create the pattern. This will help us to use sophie Germain Identity

## Worked Example

### Basic Factoring

Factor $a^4 + 4$.

Write $a^4 + 4 = a^4 + 4\cdot 1^4$ and apply the identity:
$(a^2 - 2a + 2)(a^2 + 2a + 2)$.


### Factor a Constant

Factor $16x^4 + 4y^4$.

Factor out $4$ first to match the Sophie Germain pattern, then apply the
identity.

### Binomial Substitution

Factor $(x^2+1)^2 + 4x^2$.

Treat it as $a^4+4b^4$ with $a=x^2+1$ and $b=x$.

### Integer Check

Factor $81 + 4y^4$.

Write $81 = 3^4$ and apply the identity with $x=3$.

### Is it Prime?

Your job is to find all positive integers $n$ such that the expression $n^4+4$ is prime!

We can directly apply the Sophie Germain identity here:
$(n^2-2n+2)(n^2+2n+2)$.

Now, notice that if $n>1$, then it follows that both factors must be greater than $1$, and thus the expression is COMPOSITE.
From here, checking $n=1$ gives $5$, which is indeed prime!

### Is it Composite?

Your job is to prove that $n^4+4n^2+16$ is COMPOSITE for all positive integers $n$.

First, we simply rewrite the expression in a similar way to that of Sophie Germain
$(n^2+4)^2-(2n)^2$.

Next, we can simply factor using the difference of squares:
$(n^2-2n+4)(n^2+2n+4)$.

### Diophantine Equation with Sophie-Germain flavor!

Your goal is to find ALL integer solutions to the expression: $x^4+4y^4=17$.

Again, we can simply apply the Sophie Germain identity directly:
$(x^2-2xy+2y^2)(x^2+2xy+2y^2)=17$.

It's just number theory from here, so since $17$ is prime, ONE of the factors must equal $1$. After testing small values
, we get that no such integer solutions exist.

### Representation of a Prime

Your goal is to prove that if an odd prime $p$ can be written in the following form:
$p=n^4+4m^4$, then it must be that $p=5$.

We begin by tackling via Sophie Germain as shown below:
$(n^2-2nm+2m^2)(n^2+2nm+2m^2)$.

Now, notice that because $p$ is prime, ONE of the factors factor must equal $1$, which then FORCES
$n=m=1$ and gives $p=5$.

### Is it a perfect square?

Your goal is to determine ALL positive integers $n$ such that the expression $n^4+4$ is a perfect
square.

We begin, like usual, using Sophie Germain, from which we get:
$(n^2-2n+2)(n^2+2n+2)$.

From here, it's just Number Theory. Observe that the factors differ by $4n$ and are ALMOST consecutive, so they cannot
possibly combine to form a perfect square. If we check some small cases, we will not be able to find any solutions. Note: As an exercise, rigorously prove that they cannot form a perfect square by considering consecutive squares and bounds!

### Factor...:

Your goal is to simply factor $(x^2+2)^4+4x^4$.

We can treat it as $a^4+4b^4$ with $a=x^2+2$ and $b=x$.

Applying the identity gives the following intimidating but correct expression:
$((x^2+2)^2-2x(x^2+2)+2x^2)((x^2+2)^2+2x(x^2+2)+2x^2)$.


## Strategy Checklist

- Check that the coefficient of $y^4$ is $4$ (or can be made $4$).
- Use the difference of squares view for speed.
- Substitute when the expression is a binomial square.

## Common Pitfalls

- Forgetting the coefficient $4$ is essential.
- Mistaking it for a difference of squares without the middle step.
- Applying the identity to $x^4+y^4$ without scaling.
