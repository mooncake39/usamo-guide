## Overview

Changing variables transforms roots and can make symmetric sums easier. Common
moves include shifting and taking reciprocals.
Changing variables transforms roots and can make symmetric sums way easier. For instance, instead of computing a complicated expression directly over the roots of $P(x)$, we find a new polynomial whose roots are exactly the transformed values, then apply either Vieta's formulas or other useful information given by the question to that new polynomial. Hence there are many ways for polynomial expressions that can be made. 

## Key Ideas

- If $r$ is a root of $P(x)$, then $r+k$ is a root of $P(x-k)$.
- If $r$ is a root of $P(x)$, then $1/r$ is a root of the reversed-coefficient
  polynomial when the constant term is nonzero.
- Use these to compute sums over transformed roots.
- If $r$ is a root of $P(x)$, then $1/r$ is a root of the reversed-coefficient polynomial when $a_0 \ne 0$.
- When scaling roots by $k$, one should replace $x$ with $x/k$ and make denominators simple.

## Shifting Roots

### Shift the Variable
If $r$ is a root of $P(x)$, then $r + k$ is a root of $P(x - k)$.

Replace $x$ with $x-h$ to shift roots by $h$. This is the standard way to move
between a polynomial and its translated version.
Therefore, To find the polynomial with roots $r_1 + k, r_2 + k, \ldots, r_n + k$, replace every $x$ in $P(x)$ with $x - k$.

### Reverse Coefficients for Reciprocals
$$P(x) \xrightarrow{\text{shift by } k} Q(x) = P(x - k).$$

If $P(x)=a_nx^n+\cdots+a_0$ and $a_0\ne 0$, then $x^nP(1/x)$ has roots $1/r$.
$Q(r + k) = P((r+k) - k) = P(r) = 0$.

### Use Vieta After Transformations
### Reciprocal Roots

Similarly, If $P(x) = a_n x^n + \cdots + a_0$ with $a_0 \ne 0$, the polynomial with roots $1/r_1, \ldots, 1/r_n$ can be found by reversing the coefficients. 

$$Q(x) = x^n P\!\left(\frac{1}{x}\right) = a_0 x^n + a_1 x^{n-1} + \cdots + a_n.$$

Further, creating the condition $a_0 \ne 0$ ensures none of the original roots is zero. If $a_0 = 0$, we factor out $x$ first. 

### Scaling Roots

To get roots $k \cdot r_1, k \cdot r_2, \ldots$, replace $x$ with $x/k$, and hence obtaining 
$$Q(x) = k^n \cdot P\!\left(\frac{x}{k}\right).$$

### Polynomial Equations After Transformations/Manipultaions

Once the new polynomial $Q(x)$ is found, we can apply Vieta's formulas directly from its coefficients. 

For quick computations like $\sum 1/r_i$, it is often faster to use Vieta on the original directly, 

$$\frac{1}{r} + \frac{1}{s} + \frac{1}{t} = \frac{rs + st + tr}{rst}.$$

Once the new polynomial is found, apply Vieta to compute sums and products.

## Strategy Checklist

- Decide whether you need a shift or a reciprocal.
- Apply the substitution carefully and simplify.
- Use Vieta to extract root sums/products.
- There are many forms of polynomial manipulations, shift, a reciprocal, or a scale.
- For a shift of $k$, we replace $x$ with $x - k$ and expand the polynomial.
- A lot of graphing can also be done with polynomials manipulation, for visual intuition. More about this is covered on other modules. 

## Common Pitfalls

- Shifting in the wrong direction.
- Forgetting that reciprocal roots require a nonzero constant term.
- Dropping factors when forming $x^nP(1/x)$.
- One can easily shifting the polynomial to the wrong direction. To get roots $r+k$, replace $x$ with $x-k$, and do not replace it with $x+k$.
- If the constant term is zero, factor out $x$ first.
- After a transformation, use the new polynomial's coefficients for Vieta's formulas, not the original's.
